I started off as a wildlife ecologist with a research focus in avian conservation. Being a wildlife ecologist gave me the ability to travel the world working in tropical, sub-tropical, and desert ecosystems taking me to places such as South Africa, Seychelles, North Carolina, California, Nevada, and ultimately residing in Florida. Part of being an avian ecologist involves spending a tremendous amount of effort collecting field data for analyzing and reporting. These reports started off with general qualatative and quantatative analytics using only spreadsheets and GIS software, but I always felt there was more to be uncovered in the data. This is my story.

### Life as a wildlife ecologist
I always loved being outside and animals growing up, and the fact that I could turn this passion into a career felt like recess every day.  I was trained in point-count surveys, line-distance sampling, nesting surveys, various trapping techniques like mist netting, and bird banding. 

### What sparked my interest
While working in the solar energy sector, I was introducted to an experiment called a carcass-persistence study. The purpose of this study was to identify how often a crew needs to survey for wildlife fatalities caused by the harvesting of solar energy so the Department of Energy can document its affect it has on the local populations. The way you do this is by leaving carcasses in and around the solar fields for scavengers to consume and then monitor those sites daily to determine the length of time it takes for signs of a fatality to no longer be present. There are a few ways to analyze this data, but the one this project used was using the USGS's [Fatality Estimator](https://pubs.usgs.gov/ds/729/pdf/ds729.pdf) package in R. This project was the turning point in my career and when I really started my journey as a data scientist.

### Currently in geospatial analytics
I have been using GIS software for analytics and mapping since 2009. I worked on multi-million dollar transportation projects modeling least cost paths for potential new corridors, developing applications for data collection and data visualization, and managing large-scale databases. Using python and webhooks allowed me to create custom tools to automate and schedule processes while also sending notifications to the appropriate recepients with freshly generated summary reports. I also developed and tuned machine learning models to predict the probability of incapacitating car crashes happening using models like regression, naïve bayes, random forest, and boosting using R.
